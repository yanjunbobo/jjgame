Rainbow Six Siege — Complete Guide 2026

The operator meta that actually wins ranked games, map callouts that stop your team from dying to rotate flanks, and the role-specific mistakes that separate Copper from Platinum.

The 5 Roles (And What You're Actually Supposed to Do)

Most players in Bronze-Gold have a vague sense of their role but no specific understanding of what success looks like. Here's what each role actually needs to deliver, and the specific mistake that defines failure in each.

Entry Fragger: First In, First to Die (And That's Fine)

The entry fragger's job is information gathering through aggression. You enter site first because the enemy will react to the first breach — and those reactions tell your team everything. A defenderpre-aiming a common entry point = that point is not being watched by someone else. A roamer failing to flank the entry = the roam is either dead or out of position.

The failure mode: dying without gaining information. If you push into site through a rotation hole and die without your team getting a kill from the pressure, you didn't do your job. If you push and force two defenders to turn around, and your team kills them during the rotation, you did your job even if you died. K/D means nothing for entry fraggers. Info-to-kill conversion is everything.

Best operators for entry: IQ (intel through walls), Brava (hijacks defender cameras and drones), Ace (can open hatches and deny Mira), Osa (透明盾 allows safe entry angles). In lower ranks, Ash (speed and flashes) and Twitch (drone disruption) are the reliable entry picks.

IGL: The Role Everyone Thinks They Can Do

In-game leader is the hardest role in Siege because it requires simultaneous processing of game state, opponent tendencies, and team capabilities — while other players are focusing on their own mechanics.

The most common IGL mistake: calling eco rounds when the team has a 60%+ win probability on full buy. After a 3-round loss streak, the psychological pressure to "save" equipment causes IGLs to call eco when they're mathematically better off buying. Here's the math: a won full-buy round gives you approximately $3,500 per player. A lost eco round gives you approximately $800. The difference is $2,700. You need to win 3 eco rounds to equal one won full-buy round. That math almost never works out.

What good IGL calls look like: "They're running a 3-speed flank on Border, I want a 2-3 with one holding the armory flank and two holding the main push." Specific, spatially aware, accounts for enemy tendencies. Bad IGL calls: "we should rush" — no timing, no position, no backup plan.

Lurk: The Distraction That Wins Rounds

The lurker's job is creating pressure that forces enemy rotations. If you're alive on the opposite side of the map from the push, and the enemy team hears you, at least one defender has to turn around. That one rotation is the window your team uses to get a kill.

The lurk failure mode: dying without forcing a rotation. If you push into a 1v3 on the opposite side of the map and die without making noise, you've given your team nothing. The lurk's value is almost entirely in the sound and sight of their presence. Make them look at you, then die if you have to — as long as your team capitalized on the rotation.

Best lurkers: Pulse (cardiac scanner through floors), Caviera (silent step for sneaky flanks), Mara and Tubarão (best 3-speed lurkers in the current meta), Flores (drones flush roamers before the round timer becomes a factor).

Support: Holding Angles That Win Fights

The support player's job is creating crossfires and holding angles that make the enemy choose between multiple threats. The support doesn't push — they create the angle that the entry fragger pushes into, or they hold the flank that prevents the enemy from rotating.

The support failure mode: abandoning your angle to get kills. If you're holding a defuser plant angle and you see a 1v1 opportunity on the other side of site, you take it — unless leaving your angle opens a rotation path for the enemy. The 3-second window your angle was uncovered might be exactly when the enemy pushes through it.

Best supports: Mira (creates irreversible angles on site), Smoke (denies plant and zone control), Mute (counters drone-heavy attackers), Fenrir (denies information through utility), Oryo (denies plant and has great guns).

Anti-Entry: The Job No One Wants

The anti-entry player is the last line of defense against rushes — their job is to deny the entry fragger's information gathering by killing them before they can report back. This is the highest-impact role in organized play and the most thankless in solo queue.

The anti-entry failure mode: holding too static an angle. Entry fraggers expect pre-aimed common angles. The best anti-entry defenders anticipate the entry and counter-position — holding an unexpected angle or a vertical counter from above/below.

Best anti-entry operators: Rook (armor gives you an HP advantage in the duel), Doc (self-heal keeps you in the fight), Melusi (debuffs approaching enemies), Azami (canisters block rush angles after they commit).

Map Callouts That Win Rounds

Bad callouts kill more rounds than bad aim in Siege. A team that communicates position precisely has a significant information advantage. These are the essential callout categories that every player needs to know.

Bank: Essential Callouts

Bank has four distinct floors with overlapping vertical space — vertical Callouts are critical.

  • Lobby / One Lobby: The main entrance from spawn into the ground floor. "One in Lobby" means one defender in that common holding position.
  • Tellars / CCTV: The two ground-floor site areas flanking the main lobby. Tellars is the desk area near the vault; CCTV is the camera room overlooking the lobby.
  • Ellite / 2F: The second floor offices — the most contested vertical space. "Ellite" refers to the executive office area; 2F is the open office adjacent.
  • Roof / skylight: The exterior roof accessible from stairs. Defenders hold roof to prevent attacker drone placement and vertical pressure.
  • Garage: The basement auto-shop. One of the two bomb sites, typically held with Mira and Jaeger.

The callout mistake that loses rounds: saying "one in Lobby" when you mean "one in Tellers." On Bank, these are 15 meters apart and represent completely different defender intentions. Be specific about the room name, not just the floor.

Border: Essential Callouts

Border is a linear map with two symmetrical sides — knowing which side matters in callouts.

  • Vending / Armory: The armory room on the east or west side of the building. Teams frequently forget the armory flank — a single defender holding the armory rotation can win rounds by themselves.
  • Customs / Terminal: The two main external areas. Customs is the cargo area; Terminal is the bus area adjacent to it.
  • Exit / West / East: Designates which side of the building the action is on. "Two west, one exit" means two enemies on the west side rotating through the exit hallway.
  • Workshop / 1F: The ground floor workshop room — a secondary site area frequently used in 1F strategies.

Clubhouse: Essential Callouts

Clubhouse has two bomb sites (Basement and 2F Gym) with a central contested lounge area.

  • Gym / 2F: The second floor gym — a site with multiple external walls vulnerable to hard breachers.
  • Basement / Bar: The bar area and basement — the second site, held with vertical play through the floor above.
  • Blue / Lounge: The central lounge connecting both sites. Control of lounge determines which site the attackers can safely push.
  • CC / Crew Quarters: The room adjacent to the gym — frequently used for off-site setups by roamers.
  • Church / CCTV: The external areas outside the bar and near the alarm room — key for roamers to hold exits.

The Universal Callout Rule

Every callout should answer three questions: How many, where exactly, doing what. "One in Tellers, holding Lounge,ADS on the door" is a perfect callout. "One there" is useless.

The other universal rule: call what you know, not what you guess. "I think there's someone in Tellers" and "There's definitely one in Tellers" are different callouts with different values. Your IGL makes different decisions based on confidence level. Don't overstate your certainty.

Operator Tier List for Ranked — Year 10 Season 2

Based on pick rate, win delta, and ease-of-use in Diamond and below lobbies.

S-Tier: The Meta-defining Picks

Azami — The most flexible defender in the current meta. Her circular canisters can block rush angles after attackers commit, deny plant angles post-defuser, and be used offensively to push attackers out of positions. She fits into any defender composition and her AR is one of the best in the game.

Ying — The premier site-taking operator. Her candelas flash everything in their radius, and combined with a hard breacher (Ace or Hibana), she can clear site and plant in one coordinated push. She's been top-tier for three seasons because she solves the fundamental defender advantage in site takes.

Osa — The transparent shield is one of the most versatile gadgets in the game. Used defensively to deny plant angles; used offensively as mobile cover during site entry. Her balance between gadget utility and strong primary/secondary weapons keeps her picked in every ranked game.

Brava — The best counter-intel operator. Her drones hijack defender cameras, dokkaebi calls, and朱妮's location sensors — turning defender utility against them. In Diamond+ where utility management wins rounds, Brava's value is highest.

A-Tier: Strong Picks for Ranked

Ace — The most reliable hard breacher. Can open hatches from below (preventing them from being denied by lesion or mute), has a great SMG, and his water lance opens walls that Hibana can't touch. He's the standard hard breacher pick in most compositions.

Deimos — After his recoil adjustment, Deimos is the premier early-round information gatherer. His tracker pistol marks enemies through walls for your team, and his 3-speed allows him to reposition after getting information.

Mira — The best site-holding defender. Her black mirrors create irreversible angles that attackers must deal with or concede site. In organized play she's irreplaceable; in solo queue she's still strong because defenders default to holding her angles.

Finka — The underrated entry support. Her recoil reduction and self-revive make her a strong pick in aggressive pushes. In lobbies where attackers struggle with recoil management, Finka's ability to flat-line enemy guns is disproportionately valuable.

Iana — The best intel denial operator. Her Gemini drone costs defenders time to confirm or deny, and that time pressure forces suboptimal decisions. Her AR is competitive with primary attackers, making her a strong flex pick.

B-Tier: Viable With Context

Twitch — Still the drone disruption queen, but her drone is increasingly being countered by defenders who check common drone holes. Still viable in organized stacks where drone timing is coordinated.

Hibana — The classic hard breacher. Can open all reinforced walls and hatches. Slower than Ace for wall opening, but her pellets are more flexible for opening hatches in awkward positions.

Smoke — The best plant denial in the game. His canisters force attackers to either wait them out (giving defenders time) or push through them (taking damage). Still essential on most basement sites.

Solis — The dedicated counter-drone operator. Her pistol identifies attackers through walls and jams their drones. Strong in organized play where drone timing is coordinated; weaker in solo queue where individual drone calls matter more.

C-Tier: Situationally Useful

Castle, Pulse, Tachanka, Goyo, Wamai, Fenrir — Each has a specific use case but are consistently outclassed by higher-tier picks in general ranked play. They work best in coordinated stacks with specific strategies built around their utility.

Economy System: The Math That Keeps You Competitive

R6 Siege's economy is a layered system that rewards consistent round-winning over individual performance. Understanding it mathematically is the difference between being perpetually broke and having full kits every round.

The Five Round Types

  • Full Buy ($4,500+): Armor + Primary + Secondary + all utility. You have everything you need. Take these rounds seriously — they're your best chance to win decisively.
  • Partial Buy ($3,500-$4,499): Armor + Primary + limited utility. You can compete but have a resource disadvantage if the round goes long.
  • Eco ($800-$2,000): Light armor + pistol + maybe a shotgun. You cannot win head-to-head fights against full buys unless you outposition them completely.
  • Force Buy ($2,500-$3,499): The awkward middle. Enough for armor + a decent weapon but no utility. The goal is to catch the enemy eco with your better guns before they acquire full equipment.
  • Pistol Round ($800): The first round. No armor or pistol depending on your loadout. The round-winning team gets $3,500 bonus each.

The Loss Bonus Cap — And How to Exploit It

R6 caps loss bonuses at 4 rounds. After 4 consecutive losses, you stop gaining bonus money per loss. The exploit: if you're on a 3-round loss streak, mathematically you should full buy on round 4 even if your team disagrees. Here's why: a full buy that wins gives $3,500 per player. An eco round that wins gives $800 per player. The difference is $2,700. You'd need to win 3+ eco rounds in a row to equal one won full-buy round — and at 3 losses, your team is already tilted and low on utility.

The reverse exploit: when you're on a 3-round win streak and the enemy is eco-ing, they're trying to save for a full buy on round 4. Punish this by playing aggressively and taking the fight before they have the economy to compete. Don't let them reach round 4 with full kits.

The 2-0 Opening Round Strategy

Going up 2-0 is one of the biggest psychological and economic swings in Siege. As the winning team after 2 rounds: you should be richer and mentally stronger. As the losing team, you're poorer and more likely to tilt.

The winning team's strategy after 2-0: Don't give them the eco round for free. Play aggressive on round 3 to deny their eco buy's value. Even if you lose the round, the enemy used their saved money on round 3, which means they're broke on round 4 — your comeback window.

The losing team's strategy after 0-2: Eco on round 3. Give up the round, bank $3,500 per player, and full buy round 4. One won round 4 with full kits gives you more momentum and economy than two won eco rounds.

Ranked Climb: What Actually Works

Copper-Bronze: Mechanics First

At this rank, game sense matters less than mechanics. Your job is to develop consistent aim, learn operator gadgets by playing them, and build the muscle memory for recoil control and crosshair placement.

The specific advice: play 50 games with the same operator. Not to one-trick, but to learn their gadget, their angles, their counters, and their synergies. After 50 games with Sledge, you know every floor soft wall on every map, which walls are reinforced, and which common holding positions are vulnerable to vertical play. That knowledge transfers to every other operator in the same category.

Silver-Gold: Role Clarity

This is where role confusion costs the most. In Silver-Gold, you frequently see teams with three entry fraggers and no one holding site, or two IGLs arguing about calls. The fix: explicitly assign roles before the match starts. Even in solo queue, say "I'll entry, you two support site, you lurk on the opposite flank." If everyone has a job, the chaos reduces.

The Gold trap: players at this rank know enough to make calls but not enough to make good calls. If you're the IGL, keep calls simple and spatial — "two here, hold this angle" is better than a complex 5-step strategy that requires timing your team doesn't have.

Platinum: Utility Timing

In Platinum, everyone has mechanics. The difference is utility usage and timing. A Gold player and a Platinum player with the same gun have different win rates — the difference is when they use their utility.

The utility-first mentality: before every push, identify what defender utility needs to be removed. If there's a Mira on site, your push plan must include Mira denial. If there's a Smoke, someone needs to be holding the plant angle with a pre-aim while the plant happens. Pushes that don't account for defender utility are coin flips — and coin flips don't climb.

Diamond+: The Micro-Details Matter

At Diamond, mechanics are consistent and game sense is developed. What separates Diamond from Champion is micro-decision-making under pressure: when to rotate, when to hold, when to trade, when to play for time.

The drone timing rule: you should always have one drone in position before the round timer hits 60 seconds. If you're not droning at 50 seconds, you're either already on site or you didn't plan your entry. In Diamond+, an undroned entry is a death sentence.

The Attacker-Defender Dynamic: Why Site Takes Fail

In modern Siege, defenders have significant advantages: they know the site, they choose the angles, they can reinforce, and they have roamers who delay and disrupt. Attackers need to systematically neutralize these advantages. Most ranked games are lost because attackers don't systematically remove defender tools.

The systematic site take: (1) Drone the roam — remove the lurkers before the round timer becomes a pressure. (2) Remove external defender utility — Mute jammers, Jager ADS, Wamai discs. (3) Open the walls with hard breachers. (4) Clear remaining defender utility — smoke denies, lesion mines on plant. (5) Plant and hold the plant angle.

The failure pattern: pushing site without removing Mute jammers first, so your hard breachers don't work. Or opening walls without checking for Mira angles, so your team gets peeked through the newly opened wall. Each step skipped compounds into the next failure.
