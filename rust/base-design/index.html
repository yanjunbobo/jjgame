Honeycomb engineering and the split-loot strategy

Base Design Guide — Anti-Grief Designs That Hold Up | 2026

The #1 killer of new Rust players is poorly designed bases — ones that look good but fold in 10 minutes to a coordinated 4-man raid. The #1 mistake intermediate players make is over-investing in external TCs instead of internal defense. Here's the engineering approach to bases that actually survive.

📊 The 3 Core Design Principles

Principle 1: Layered Defense (Honeycomb)
The minimum viable base has 3 honeycomb layers before your loot room. Each door = 1 layer. A 2-door airlock + 1 door to TC + 1 door to loot = 4 layers minimum. More layers = more explosives required. Every 2 walls of sheet metal = 1 additional satchel charge. A 4-layer base requires 8 satchels = 40 explosives total. A 2-layer base requires 4 satchels = trivial.
Principle 2: No Inline TC Placement
Your TC (tool cupboard) determines the build privilege radius. If your TC is inline with your walls (visible from outside), enemies can wall off from outside your TC radius and build into your base from an unprotected angle. Place your TC in a corner that's ONLY accessible from inside your base — never visible from exterior walls.
Principle 3: The 1x1 Mistake
The classic 1x1 base (1 twig + 1 stone wall) takes 1 satchel to raid. Cost to build: 200 stones. Cost to raid: 1 satchel (4 explosive 5.56 or 1 rocket). Every 1x1 base is a net loss. The minimum viable base: a 2x2 with 2 honeycomb layers. This requires 4 satchels = the enemy needs 800 explosives to guarantee entry. Your 400-rock base now requires 2,400 materials to raid — and they might quit first.

⚠️ The 5 Base Building Mistakes

Mistake 1: Building Near Monuments
Airfield, Dome, and Launch Site are high-traffic areas. Building within 200 meters of these = guaranteed daily raids from roamers. The ideal base location is 400-600m from nearest monument, near water, on a rock or hill for visibility. You're visible on terrain maps — pick flat ground or cliffs to break your silhouette.
Mistake 2: Not Building a Decoy Base
Top players build 2 bases: a decoy base (near monument, cheap) and a real base (far away, expensive). The decoy soaks raids from random roamers. Your real base only gets hit by targeted players who have spent time scouting — and if they've done that, a decoy won't help anyway. Build the decoy with half your resources on wipe day.
Mistake 3: Using Garage Doors as Primary Defense
Garage doors have a 4-second open/close animation. During those 4 seconds, anyone can walk in. Sheet metal doors close instantly. Only use garage doors for vehicle bays — never for primary base entry.
Mistake 4: Single-Point-of-Failure Loot Room
If your entire base has 1 entry to the loot room, it's trivially easy to raid. Split your loot: main base holds tools and components, secondary hidden base (a 1x1 at a different location on the map) holds valuables. This is called "splitting the egg" — one base might get raided but you never lose everything.
Mistake 5: Not Upgrading Before Storing
Store only upgraded materials in your base. If you have sheet metal walls but wood floors, the enemy destroys the floors first (30 seconds with a hatchet), then shoots through the gap. Always upgrade floors, then walls, then doors. Priority: loot room first.
